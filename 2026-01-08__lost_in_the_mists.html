The road into and out of the Village of Barovia is narrow and unwelcoming, a gray ribbon of mud that sticks at your boots that winds between skeletal trees and low, choking fog. Some of you have walked these roads only hours. Others have learned their weight over days or weeks. None of you would claim to truly know them. At first, the path seems certain. Ruts in the dirt. Broken branches bent by passing wagons. Milestones half-swallowed by moss and rot. The forest looms close, but the road is still there, guiding, patient. Then the fog thickens. It does not roll in all at once. It creeps. It curls low around boots and wheels, clings tight, dulls sound. Distances shorten. Landmarks blur. The road bends more sharply than expected, then again, then again. Familiar shapes repeat in ways that feel wrong -- the same leaning tree, the same split stone, the same patch of dead brush. Time stretches. Direction loses meaning. Every attempt to orient yourselves seems to end the same way: back where you thought you had already been. The forest presses closer, branches clawing overhead like ribs closing around a cage. The air is cold now. Damp. Quiet in a way that feels deliberate. Somewhere along the road, through the fog and the trees, other figures emerge. Travelers. Shapes resolving into faces, armor, cloaks, silhouettes. Strangers, each as lost as the next, drawn together not by choice, but by the simple fact that the road refuses to let any of you go alone. The mist thickens further, swallowing the road behind you. Ahead, it offers no promise of clearing. For now, the forest watches. Silent. Waiting.
